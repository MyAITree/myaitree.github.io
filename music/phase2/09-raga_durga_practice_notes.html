Raga Durga
Comprehensive Practice Notes
"Beguiling in its pentatonic simplicity, Durga is inextricably tied to visions of the Hindu Mother Goddess — depicted in lore as destroyer of demons and protector of the faithful. The raga carries both her fierce power and her devotional sweetness: Veer and Bhakti in a single melodic breath." — After Ragajunglism; Grokipedia; Songs of Yore
01Parichay (परिचय) — Introduction

Raga Durga is a pentatonic (audav-audav) raga belonging to the Bilawal Thaat. Its name derives from the Sanskrit word meaning "invincible, impassable, inaccessible" — precisely the epithets of the Hindu Mother Goddess Maa Durga, the destroyer of the demon Mahishasura. The raga carries both aspects of the goddess: fierce devotional power (Veer Rasa) and tender, protective love (Bhakti Rasa).

Durga uses only five notes — Sa, Re, Ma, Pa, Dha — all shuddha (natural). Gandhar and Nishad are completely omitted. This pentatonic structure makes it a natural companion to Bhupali (which omits Ma and Ni), and the two ragas are frequently studied together to understand how different pentatonic selections from the same thaat family create entirely different emotional worlds.

The most important fact about Durga's avroh: The descending scale is not a simple reversal of the ascent. Durga uses vakra (oblique) movement in the avroh — specifically, Dha appears twice in characteristic descent patterns: S' Dha Pa Ma, and then again Dha Ma Re Dha Sa. Pancham (Pa) is not a resting note in the avroh — it is a passing note. The avroh's primary resting points are Sa, Ma, and Dha. Getting this right is the key to Durga.

04Aaroh–Avroh aur Pakad
Aaroh (आरोह) — Ascending Sā Re Ma Pa Dha Sā' (Ga and Ni absent; clean pentatonic ascent; Ma is the emotional centre)
Avroh (अवरोह) — Descending (with vakra Dha) Sā' Dha Pa Ma , Pa Dha Ma , Re Dha Sā (Pa is alpa — never rest on it in descent; Dha appears twice — this vakra quality is idiosyncratic)
The vakra avroh explained: The full avroh pattern includes:
• S' Dha Pa Ma — Dha moving down through Pa to Ma
• Pa Dha Ma — Pa moves up to Dha before coming back down to Ma (the vakra gesture)
• Re Dha Sa — Re drops to lower Dha before resolving on Sa (the most identifying phrase of Durga)
Pa is used as a passing note only — phrases never rest on Pa going down.
Pakad (पकड़) — Characteristic Catch-phrase Re Ma Pa Dha , Ma Pa Dha , Ma Re , Sa Re Dha Sa
The most identifying phrase of Durga — Sa Re Dha Sa Sa Re .Dha Sa , Sa Re .Dha Sa (.Dha = lower octave Dha; this phrase — Re dropping to lower Dha, then Sa — is uniquely Durga's signature)

Key melodic rules

  • Ma is the vadi — the gravitational centre; phrases radiate from and resolve to Ma
  • Pa is alpa in avroh — never rest on Pa going down; it is a quick passing note between Dha and Ma
  • Dha appears twice in characteristic descent — Pa Dha Ma (vakra upward touch) is idiosyncratic to Durga
  • Sa Re Dha Sa — the most identifying phrase; Re drops to lower Dha then Sa; practise daily
  • Three nyaas swaras — Sa, Ma, and Dha; phrases naturally pause on these three notes
  • All notes shuddha — no komal, no teevra; Durga's simplicity is its most challenging aspect

Character and feel

  • Durga has the same notes as Shuddha Malhar — but Re's role distinguishes them; Durga emphasises Re prominently while Malhar de-emphasises it
  • The Re–Pa sangati (interval) has a folk-like directness; the raga is "accessible" without being simple
  • Both Veer (heroic) and Bhakti (devotional) rasas are present — the raga can project strength or surrender depending on treatment
  • The characteristic Sa Re Dha Sa phrase gives Durga a distinctive "circular" quality — it returns on itself
  • Durga is purvanga-pradhan — most melodic activity centred below Pa
  • Ustad Amjad Ali Khan particularly favours this raga; Bhimsen Joshi used it frequently in school
07Alankars (अलंकार) — Scale Exercises

Practise with tanpura drone. Focus on: (1) no Ga or Ni ever, (2) Pa as alpa/transient in descent, (3) Sa Re Dha Sa as the signature phrase, (4) the vakra Pa Dha Ma pattern.

11Taan Prakar (तान प्रकार) — Types of Taans
All taans in Durga must maintain: no Ga or Ni; Pa alpa in descent (no resting); vakra Dha pattern in descending runs; Sa Re Dha Sa as a possible cadential phrase.

S R M P D S' | S' D P M R S (note: no Ga or Ni anywhere)

12Durga, Bhupali, and the Pentatonic Cousins

Durga, Bhupali, and Malhar/Megh all share pentatonic structures from the all-shuddha note set. Comparing them reveals how radically different emotional worlds can be built from closely related material.

Raga Durga 🌙
Sa Re Ma Pa Dha
Thaat: Bilawal. Vadi: Ma. Night (9 PM–12). Veer + Bhakti. Signature: Sa Re .Dha Sa. Pa is alpa in avroh. Vakra Dha in descent.
Raga Bhupali 🌅
Sa Re Ga Pa Dha
Thaat: Kalyan. Vadi: Ga. Evening (6–9 PM). Shant + Bhakti. Uses Ga; omits Ma and Ni. Begins from lower .Dha. Serene, domestic.
Raga Malhar 🌧️
Sa Re Ma Pa Dha
Same notes as Durga! Distinguished by treatment: Malhar de-emphasises Re (Re–Pa sangati replaces Re-Ma). Monsoon raga. Different chalan entirely.
Durga vs Malhar — the most important distinction: Both ragas use the identical five notes (Sa Re Ma Pa Dha). They are distinguished primarily by the treatment of Re. Durga prominently features Re throughout and uses the Sa Re .Dha Sa phrase as its signature. Malhar de-emphasises Re and uses a Re–Pa sangati differently. The aural experience is completely different despite identical note sets.

Durga vs Bhupali

  • Durga: Sa Re Ma Pa Dha (omits Ga and Ni)
  • Bhupali: Sa Re Ga Pa Dha (omits Ma and Ni)
  • Durga: Bilawal Thaat; Bhupali: Kalyan Thaat
  • Durga: vadi Ma, night raga; Bhupali: vadi Ga, evening raga
  • Durga: Veer + Bhakti; Bhupali: Shant + gentle devotion
  • Quickest test: if Ma is present → Durga; if Ga is present → Bhupali

Durga vs Shuddha Saveri (Carnatic)

  • Both use the same five notes: Sa Re Ma Pa Dha
  • Different sancharas (movements) entirely
  • Shuddha Saveri's rishabh may use a slightly different shruti (microtone)
  • Shuddha Saveri is performed at any time in Carnatic; Durga is a night raga
  • The emotional character differs significantly between traditions
  • Durga has the vakra Dha pattern; Shuddha Saveri does not have a direct equivalent
13Rasa aur Bhaav (रस और भाव) — Emotion & Mood
Veer Rasa — वीर
Heroic strength, valor, and invincibility — the primary masculine quality of Durga, named after the warrior goddess. The raga has an upward directness in its ascent and a poised authority in its descent that conveys this quality naturally.
Bhakti — भक्ति
Deep devotion to the goddess Durga — the protective, nurturing dimension of the deity. Many kirtans and stotras (hymns to Durga) are set in this raga, expressing both awe and surrender before the divine mother.
Shringaar — श्रृंगार
Wikipedia cites Shringaar as the rasa of Durga — romantic love, particularly in the lighter bandish and thumri contexts. The raga's pentatonic clarity and the sweetness of the Ma–Dha interval lend themselves to romantic expression.
Soothing Night — शांत रात्रि
The raga evokes a soothing, devotional nocturnal mood. Unlike the anxious darkness of Marwa or the yearning of Bageshri, Durga's night is assured and powerful — the silence of a warrior at rest.

15Carnatic Connection — Shuddha Saveri

Shuddha Saveri shares the same five notes as Raga Durga (Sa Re Ma Pa Dha / C D F G A) but belongs to a completely different musical tradition with different chalan, ornaments, and emotional character. The equivalence is approximate — Ragajunglism notes the sancharas are "significantly different."

18Practice Schedule (अभ्यास क्रम)

Daily Practice — 45 Minutes

  • 0–5 min: Tanpura setup; establish Ma as the gravitational centre of the drone
  • 5–10 min: Alankar 1 — core aaroh–avroh; focus on Pa alpa and vakra Dha in descent
  • 10–15 min: Alankars 2–4 — step patterns; Sa Re Dha Sa signature phrase
  • 15–25 min: Alaap phrases 1–3; Sa Re Dha Sa opening; Ma as resting note
  • 25–35 min: Alaap phrases 4–6; double Dha phrases; taar exploration; full resolution
  • 35–40 min: Sargam geet with Teentaal

19Common Mistakes to Avoid
MistakeCorrection
Using Ga or NiBoth Gandhar and Nishad are strictly varjit (forbidden) in Durga. Any appearance of Ga or Ni immediately destroys the raga's identity. There is no exception to this rule.
Using Teevra Ma (F♯)Durga is a Bilawal thaat raga — all-shuddha. The teevra Madhyam (F♯) must never appear. This would shift the raga into the Kalyan family entirely.
Resting on Pa in the avrohPa is alpa (transient) in the descending scale — it is passed through, never lingered on. Resting on Pa in descent gives the raga a completely wrong shape. The nyaas swaras are Sa, Ma, and Dha — not Pa.
Skipping the vakra Dha patternThe Pa Dha Ma vakra movement in descent is idiosyncratic to Durga. Not using it makes a descending phrase sound generic. Similarly, the double Dha phrase (D D M) is characteristic and should appear regularly.
Not using the Sa Re Dha Sa phraseThis is Durga's most identifying single phrase. Its absence makes the raga sound like a scale exercise rather than Raga Durga. Practise it until it appears naturally in alaap and taan.
Confusing with BhupaliBhupali uses Ga; Durga uses Ma. If Ga appears, it is Bhupali. If Ma appears without Ga, it is Durga. The two ragas are also in different thaats (Kalyan vs Bilawal) and different time slots (evening vs night).
Confusing with MalharSame notes as Durga (Sa Re Ma Pa Dha) but completely different treatment. Malhar de-emphasises Re; Durga features Re prominently. Listen carefully to the Sa Re .Dha Sa phrase — this is Durga's signature; it does not appear in Malhar.
