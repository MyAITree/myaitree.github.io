Raga Kedar
राग केदार — Comprehensive Practice Notes
"Kedar occupies a high pedestal in Indian classical music. Named after Lord Shiva — ruler of Kedarnath — it has the unique distinction of using both shuddha and teevra Madhyam in the same raga. The meend from Dha to Ma via Pa is the very heart of this raga." — After Wikipedia; Parrikar Archive; Artium Academy; WholisticWellnessSpace
01Parichay (परिचय)

Raga Kedar (also Kedara) is one of the most revered and intricate ragas in Hindustani classical music, belonging to the Kalyan Thaat. It is named after Lord Shiva — whose most sacred dwelling is Kedarnath in the Himalayas — and carries the gravity, majesty, and spiritual elevation associated with the deity.

Kedar's defining and unique characteristic is its use of both shuddha Madhyam (F♮) and teevra Madhyam (F♯) within the same raga — making it one of the rare ragas that employs two forms of a single note. Its melodic movement (chalan) is highly vakra (oblique/zigzag) — a straightforward scalar rendition sounds nothing like Kedar; mastering the raga requires internalising its characteristic phrase patterns rather than its scale.

Kedar and the Kalyan Panchak: Kedar is one of five ragas forming the prestigious "Kalyan Panchak" or Panchratna — five jewel ragas of the Kalyan family: Kedar, Hameer, Gaud Sarang, Kamod, and Chhayanat. All five use shuddha swaras plus teevra Ma, but each has a completely distinct character. Learning to tell these five apart is a major milestone in Hindustani classical education.

Shuddha Ma (M) = F♮ — the VADI

The primary note of Kedar. Phrases rest on shuddha Ma. The ascending line returns to M frequently. Shuddha Ma defines the raga's emotional centre. In the aaroh: Sa M (G)m P — shuddha Ma is the launching note. In the avroh: m P M R S — shuddha Ma appears as the resting point before descending to Re.

Teevra Ma (m) = F♯ — the expressive agent

Appears in characteristic phrases and the defining meend. The meend from Dha to shuddha Ma via Pa (D → P → m → M) traverses teevra Ma on the way. Teevra Ma also appears in the phrase m P m P (alternating with Pa) which is idiosyncratic to Kedar. Never a resting note; always a passing or meend note.

The notation convention: M (capital) = shuddha Madhyam (F♮). m (lowercase) = teevra Madhyam (F♯). This is opposite to the general Hindustani notation where lowercase = komal. In Kedar specifically: M = shuddha Ma (vadi); m = teevra Ma. This convention is used throughout these notes.
04Aaroh–Avroh aur Pakad
Aaroh (आरोह) — Ascending · 5–6 notes · Re absent · Ga as grace note only Sā M (G)m Pa Dha Ni Sā' (M = shuddha Ma; (G) = Gandhar as subtle grace note before teevra m; Re absent; sometimes: S M M P D N S')
Avroh (अवरोह) — Descending · all 7 notes · defining meend Sā' Ni Dha Pa m Pa M Re Sā (the phrase D → Pa → m → Pa → M = the heart of Kedar; meend from Dha through Pa to shuddha Ma)
The defining meend — the heart of Kedar: The phrase D → P → m → M (Dha descending through Pa, touching teevra Ma, then landing on shuddha Ma) is described as "the heart of the raga — repeated again and again." The slide traverses two forms of Madhyam in a single meend. This is Kedar's most immediately identifying gesture. Without it, the raga loses its identity.
Pakad (पकड़) S M , M P , m P M , D P M , R S (S M = the opening; m P M = the core alternation; D P M = the defining descent; R S = cadence)
Key characteristic phrases (chalan patterns) S M , S R S M , M–M P , M (G)m P M , m P m P , D P m P M , P M R S (m P m P = the alternation; D P m P M = the defining descent; S M repeated openings)
Ga is a kan swara — never a resting note: Gandhar (Ga) is extremely weak in Kedar — described as "rarely used" and only as "a grace note in the transition from Ma to Pa." The notation is (G)m before Pa — a subtle, felt-rather-than-heard embellishment. Singing Ga as a resting note immediately destroys Kedar's character.

Key melodic rules

  • M (shuddha Ma) is the vadi — phrases frequently rest on M; it is repeated for emphasis (M–M)
  • Ga is a grace note only — never a resting note; appears as (G) before teevra m
  • Re is omitted in aaroh; appears briefly in descent (m P M R S)
  • The meend D → P → m → M is the defining phrase — practise it until it is effortless
  • The alternation m P m P (teevra Ma and Pa) is idiosyncratic to Kedar
  • The phrase S M M P (shuddha Ma dwelt upon, then jumping to Pa) creates Kedar's vast, soaring quality
  • Teevra Ma (m) is always a passing or meend note — never a resting note

Vakra chalan — key patterns

  • S M , M–M P — the opening; double Ma before leaping to Pa
  • M (G)m P m P — grace Ga + teevra Ma alternation with Pa
  • D P m P M — the defining descent via teevra Ma to shuddha Ma
  • P D P P S' — the launch into taar saptak via doubled Pa
  • S' (N)R' S' — upper octave turn with Ni grace note
  • S' N D P — descent from taar into the defining D → M meend
  • P m M R S — final resolution to samvadi Sa
07Alankars (अलंकार)

Practise slowly with tanpura drone. Focus: M (shuddha Ma) as resting note; Ga as grace note only; teevra m as passing/meend note; D → P → m → M meend as the primary gesture.

ALANKAR 1 — Core aaroh–avroh
S M (G)m Pa Dha Ni S' | S' Ni Dha Pa m Pa M Re S (Re absent ascending; Ga grace note before m; D P m P M = the defining descent)
ALANKAR 2 — Repeated Ma (shuddha) and Pa leap
S M – M Pa , M M Pa Dha , Pa Dha Ni S' (M–M before Pa = the "soaring leap" quality; double shuddha Ma is characteristic)
ALANKAR 3 — Teevra Ma alternation m P m P
M (G)m Pa m Pa , m Pa m Pa Dha , Dha Pa m Pa M R S (m P m P = the idiosyncratic Kedar alternation; always ending on shuddha M)
ALANKAR 4 — The defining meend D → P → m → M
Dha ~~~ Pa ~~~ m ~~~ Pa ~~~ M – R S (~~~ = slow meend; this phrase D P m P M must be practised until completely smooth)
ALANKAR 5 — Taar saptak approach P D P P S'
M Pa Dha Pa Pa S' , S' Ni Dha Pa m Pa M R S (P D P P S' = the doubled Pa before taar jump; characteristic launch into upper octave)
ALANKAR 6 — Full pakad cell (practise daily)
S M , M Pa , (G)m Pa M , Dha Pa m Pa M , R S (the complete Kedar pakad — learn this before anything else)

11Taan Prakar (तान प्रकार)
All taans must: (1) keep Re and Ga weak/absent in ascending runs, (2) use the defining D P m P M meend, (3) always land on shuddha M as the resting point, (4) preserve the m P m P alternation as characteristic.

12The Kalyan Panchak — Telling Kedar Apart

Kedar belongs to the Kalyan Panchak — five jewel ragas of the Kalyan family, all using shuddha swaras + teevra Ma. Distinguishing them is a major milestone in Hindustani classical education.

Kedar
S M (G)m P D N S'
Vadi: Ma. Re + Ga weak/absent in aaroh. Ga as grace note. Meend D → P → m → M is the heart. Both Ma variants. Evening.
Hameer
S G M D N S' (Re absent)
Vadi: Dha. Ga prominent in aaroh (unlike Kedar). Pakad: m+P G M D. Characteristic Re–Pa meend. Night.
Gaud Sarang
S R G M P D N S'
Vadi: Re. All 7 notes in aaroh (unlike Kedar). Re is prominent. Day raga (noon). Lighter character.
Kamod
S G M D N S' (Pa-centric)
Vadi: Pa (or Ga). Characteristic Re–Pa meend (like Hameer but different treatment). Night raga.
Chhayanat
S R G m P D N S'
Vadi: Pa. Uses primarily teevra Ma (unlike Kedar's shuddha Ma vadi). Night. Elaborate vakra patterns.
Kedar vs Hameer — fastest test
Ga in aaroh = key
If Ga is prominent in ascent → Hameer. If Ga is absent/grace note → Kedar. Also: Kedar's shuddha Ma is the vadi; Hameer's vadi is Dha.

Kedar vs Yaman

  • Both Kalyan thaat; both use teevra Ma
  • Yaman: only teevra Ma; Kedar: both shuddha and teevra Ma
  • Yaman aaroh begins from lower Ni; Kedar begins from Sa
  • Yaman: vadi Ga; Kedar: vadi shuddha Ma
  • Yaman: evening/night; Kedar: evening/night
  • Yaman has a regular scale feel; Kedar is dramatically vakra

Shuddha Kedar — the variant

  • Shuddha Kedar de-emphasises meends and teevra Ma
  • It brightens the shuddha Ni in the ascent
  • More austere and direct than standard Kedar
  • Pandit Bhimsen Joshi has a celebrated recording of Shuddha Kedar
  • Think of it as Kedar without the characteristic meend complexity
  • Closer to the Carnatic Hameer Kalyani in feel
13Rasa aur Bhaav (रस और भाव)
Mixed / Chanchal — unique among ragas
Kedar has no single defining rasa. WholisticWellnessSpace notes: "One cannot define a definite feeling — like loneliness, love, separation, happiness — there is no one feeling. It has a mixture of all these feelings." This emotional multiplicity is unique.
Shiva Bhakti — devotional gravity
Named after Lord Shiva and Kedarnath — the highest of the 12 Jyotirlingas. The raga carries the gravity, majesty, and spiritual elevation associated with this deity. Devotional compositions in Kedar have a particular gravitas.
Shringaar — romantic longing
The famous bandish "Jane de mohe tori piharwa na manoon" captures the romantic dimension — a refusal to leave the beloved's home. This yearning shringaar coexists with the raga's spiritual weight.
Varsha / Monsoon
Kedar is sung mostly in the rainy season — no specific seasonal restriction, but its association with monsoon imagery and post-sunset performance gives it a characteristic atmospheric mood.

Kedar is a night raga — first prahar of the night (6–9 PM) is most commonly cited; some sources extend to 9 PM–midnight. Associated with the rainy season. The raga's majestic, multi-faceted emotional world suits the evening hour — neither the pure contemplation of midnight nor the charged energy of morning.

18Practice Schedule

  • 18–28 min: Alaap phrases 1–3; shuddha Ma as resting note; introducing teevra m in phrases

19Common Mistakes to Avoid
MistakeCorrection
Using teevra Ma (m) as a resting noteTeevra Ma is always a passing or meend note in Kedar — never a point of rest. The vadi and resting note is shuddha Ma (M). Landing on teevra m and pausing there is the most common error in Kedar.
Singing Ga as a resting noteGandhar is extremely weak in Kedar — it appears only as a subtle grace note (kan) before teevra m in the phrase (G)m P. Making Ga a full note or resting on it immediately turns the raga toward Hameer or Yaman.
Using Re prominently in the aarohRe is absent or extremely weak in Kedar's ascending line. It appears briefly in descent (m P M R S). Prominently ascending through Re sounds like Yaman or Kalyan, not Kedar.
Neglecting the D P m P M meendThis is the single most identifying phrase of Kedar — described as "the heart of the raga, repeated again and again." Not using it makes a Kedar performance unrecognisable. Practise this meend until it is completely fluent.
Skipping the M–M P (double Ma) openingThe phrase S M M P — shuddha Ma dwelt upon and repeated before leaping to Pa — creates Kedar's characteristic soaring quality. Without this doubled Ma, the raga loses its majestic character.
Confusing with HameerHameer has Ga prominent in the ascending line; Kedar does not. Also: Hameer's vadi is Dha; Kedar's vadi is shuddha Ma. The characteristic Hameer pakad phrase is m+P G M D — very different from Kedar.
Playing Kedar as a scale exerciseKedar's chalan is highly vakra — a straightforward scale rendition sounds nothing like the raga. The raga must be learned through its characteristic phrases (M–M P, m P m P, D P m P M), not its aaroh–avroh.
