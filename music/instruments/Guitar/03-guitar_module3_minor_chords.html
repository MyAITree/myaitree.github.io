L-01 What Are Minor Chords? Theory

Every chord you've played so far — D, A, E — is a major chord. Major chords sound bright, confident, happy. Minor chords have a lowered third degree, which creates a darker, more emotional, melancholic quality. The difference between a major and minor chord is literally one note — moved by just one fret.

Major chords — the sound

Bright, confident, uplifting, resolved. The classic "happy" chord sound. D major, A major, E major. Used in celebrations, anthems, upbeat Bollywood numbers. Think: Balam Pichkari, Shake It Off.

Minor chords — the sound

Dark, yearning, emotional, unresolved. The "sad" or "serious" chord sound. Em, Am. Used in love songs, film tension, soulful qawwalis. Think: Tum Hi Ho (Em), Shape of You (Em), Stairway to Heaven (Am).

The One-Note Difference — Major vs Minor E major : E — G# — B (the major third is G# / fret 1 on G string) E minor : E — G — B (the minor third is G♮ / open G string — remove finger 1) A major : A — C# — E (middle finger and ring finger on fret 2) A minor : A — C — E (finger 2 moves from D-str to B-str, finger 3 stays on D-str) The rule: flatten (lower by one fret) the middle note of any major chord → minor chord
Why this matters immediately: Em and Am are the most common minor chords in guitar music. Adding them to D, A, E gives you access to a huge new range of Bollywood emotional songs, Western ballads, and the most popular chord progression in modern music: Am · F · C · G (or its relatives). You'll recognise this progression in hundreds of songs the moment you hear it.

Most common chord progressions with minors

  • Am → F → C → G — the most used progression in pop (Bollywood and Western)
  • Em → C → G → D — "the four-chord song" (Axis of Awesome)
  • Am → Em → D — classic Bollywood minor feel
  • E → Am — the sudden minor shift creates instant drama
  • D → Em → A — common in Indian folk and classical-inspired film songs

Minor chord theory — keep it simple for now

  • Major = root + major 3rd + perfect 5th (bright)
  • Minor = root + minor 3rd + perfect 5th (darker)
  • The minor 3rd is 1 semitone (1 fret) lower than the major 3rd
  • You don't need to memorise intervals — just feel the difference
  • Play E major, then E minor, back and forth — hear how the mood shifts
  • That emotional shift is what minor chords bring to every song
L-02 The E Minor Chord New Chord

E minor is one of the easiest chords on guitar — it requires only two fingers, uses all 6 strings, and sounds immediately rich and full. It is derived directly from E major by simply removing the index finger.

The magic shortcut: E major → E minor = simply lift your index finger off the G string. The rest of the chord stays exactly the same. This also means E → Em is one of the fastest chord changes in all of guitar — zero repositioning needed for the other two fingers.

Em — what to practice first

  • Alternate E and Em: form E, lift index, strum Em, replace index, strum E
  • Feel the emotional shift — from bright E major to melancholic Em
  • Practice E → Em OMC: the change score should immediately exceed 60+/min
  • Practice Em → Am (the next chord) — this is the harder new change
  • Strum all 6 strings on Em — the open G string is the key note that creates the minor feel

Songs that use Em prominently

  • Tum Hi Ho (Aashiqui 2) — Em is the emotional heart of this song
  • Shape of You (Ed Sheeran) — Em A D A loop throughout
  • Stairway to Heaven (Led Zeppelin) — Em in the famous intro
  • Hotel California (Eagles) — Bm F# A E G D Em F# loop
  • Channa Mereya (Ae Dil Hai Mushkil) — Em based progression
  • Wonderwall (Oasis) — Em7 throughout (just Em with capo)
L-03 The A Minor Chord New Chord

A minor is the second essential minor chord. Unlike Em, it requires a new finger shape — but it has a beautiful relationship with E major: the Am shape is the same pattern of fingers as E major, just shifted to different strings. Once you see this, both chords become easier.

A Major (review)

Am is the E major shape shifted: Look at the Am fingering — index on B-str fret 1, middle on D-str fret 2, ring on G-str fret 2. Now compare to E major — index on G-str fret 1, middle on A-str fret 2, ring on D-str fret 2. The same three-finger shape, moved one string toward the higher strings. This is the "CAGED system" in action — you'll understand it fully in Module 5.

Am — key technique points

  • Index finger (1) on B string fret 1 — very close to the metal fret
  • Middle (2) and ring (3) on D and G strings fret 2 — same as the A major shape
  • High e string must ring open — if muted, index is too flat on B string
  • Mute the low E string — start the strum from the A string (string 5)
  • Am is one of the most emotionally resonant chords in guitar music

The four new OMC pairs — Module 3

  • Em ↔ Am — the main minor pair; practice daily
  • Am ↔ E — major to relative minor shift; very common in songs
  • Em ↔ D — used in hundreds of Bollywood songs
  • Am ↔ D — another very common Bollywood pair
  • Am ↔ G — key progression for Module 4 prep
  • Em ↔ G — needed for many Western folk songs
L-04 Holding The Guitar Posture

Good posture is not about looking professional — it is about preventing pain and building technique that works at speed. Poor posture creates tension that caps your playing ability and can cause real repetitive strain injury over months of practice. This lesson checks and corrects the fundamentals.

✓ Sitting position — correct

  • Guitar body rests on the right thigh (right-handed players)
  • Guitar tilted slightly toward you — not lying flat
  • The neck points slightly upward (30–45°) — not horizontal
  • Back straight — not hunched over the guitar
  • Strumming elbow rests lightly on the guitar body
  • Fretting wrist curves outward — not bent inward under the neck
  • Thumb of fretting hand sits behind the neck — 2nd/3rd fret area, opposite the middle finger

✗ Common posture errors

  • Tilting the guitar up to see the fretboard — the neck drops and tension increases
  • Squeezing the neck with the thumb hooked over the top — kills finger arch
  • Hunching to watch the left hand — causes neck and back strain over time
  • Guitar sliding on the leg — use a strap even when sitting to stabilise
  • Wrist bent sharply inward under the neck — creates carpal tunnel risk
  • Right arm pressing the guitar body away — guitar must feel secure without gripping
The strap rule: Use a strap when sitting and standing. When sitting, the strap prevents the guitar from shifting position as you change chords. When standing, proper strap height is crucial — the guitar should sit at the same height as when sitting, not dropped to the hip. Dropping the guitar to look cool creates wrist angle problems that hurt your technique.

Fretting hand position check

  • Thumb: behind the neck, roughly opposite the 2nd finger, not hooked over
  • Palm: a gap between the palm and the neck — the guitar doesn't sit in your palm
  • Fingers: arched, pressing with tips, not flat pads
  • Knuckles: roughly parallel to the fretboard
  • Wrist: curves outward — imagine holding a tennis ball under your palm
  • Tension check: can you wiggle each finger independently? If not, relax

Strumming hand position check

  • Forearm rests on the body of the guitar — a light, natural anchor point
  • Wrist hangs loose and relaxed over the sound hole
  • The strum motion is from the wrist, not the elbow or shoulder
  • Pick (or thumb) held lightly — you should be able to feel it could slip
  • After each strum, the wrist returns to its neutral position — no locking
  • The strumming hand should feel like it's doing very little — lightness is correct
L-05 Counting Ands Rhythm

To play up-strums, you must understand what happens between the beats. The "and" is the subdivision — the second eighth note in each beat. Counting "1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and" out loud is the single most powerful rhythmic tool a beginner can use.

The counting system — beats and ands

2beat
&and
3beat
&and
4beat

Purple = beat (foot down). Teal = "and" (foot up). Eight equal divisions of one bar of 4/4.

Strum-Counting Alignment — Downs on beats, Ups on ands Count : 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & Foot : ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ (never stops) Strum : ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ (all 8 eighth notes) or : ↓ · ↓ ↑ ↑ ↓ ↑ · (selective strums — DDUUDU) Rule: DOWN strums happen on beats (when foot is going DOWN) UP strums happen on ands (when foot is coming UP)

How to train counting ands

  • Step 1: Clap on every "1" while counting aloud — feel one beat per bar
  • Step 2: Clap on "1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and" — feel all 8 subdivisions
  • Step 3: Clap only on "ands" while counting aloud — isolate the upbeat
  • Step 4: Pick up guitar, hold chord, strum down on beats, up on ands
  • Step 5: Selectively drop some strums (ghost) — the pendulum keeps swinging
  • Count aloud every time you practice a new pattern — until it's internal

Ands in Bollywood context

  • Most Bollywood guitar patterns are 8th-note based — the "and" is everywhere
  • The characteristic D-DU-UDU Bollywood feel = deliberate placement of ands
  • Hindi film songs often accent the "and" of beat 2 — creating the forward push
  • Classical Indian music has its own tal system — ands correspond to matra subdivisions
  • Counting "1 aur 2 aur 3 aur 4 aur" in Hindi works just as well as "and"
L-06 All About Up Strums Technique

Up strums are the single technique that most transforms a beginner guitarist into someone who sounds musical. Without up strums, you can only play stiff quarter-note rhythms. With up strums, you have the rhythmic vocabulary of nearly every style of music.

The key insight: An up strum only catches the top 2–3 strings (high e, B, sometimes G). This is correct and intentional — not a mistake. If your up strum catches all 6 strings, it sounds harsh and unbalanced. Think of the up strum as a light, quick flick catching only the brightest strings.

Up strum mechanics — step by step

  • Your wrist is already rotating for the downstroke — the upstroke is the return
  • After the downstroke, let the wrist continue its rotation upward
  • The pick flips slightly — now the opposite face of the pick leads
  • Catch only the high strings (e and B, maybe G) on the way up
  • The upstroke should be faster and lighter than the downstroke — natural feel
  • Practice: down-up, down-up, slow with one chord, count "1 and 2 and"

Ghost strums vs real up strums

  • Ghost strum = hand moves upward but DOES NOT touch the strings
  • Real up strum = hand moves upward AND catches the high strings
  • Both are valid — the difference is whether you want sound at that "and"
  • Pattern reading: ↑ = real up strum · · = ghost strum (hand moves, no sound)
  • The pendulum always swings both ways — only the contact point creates sound
  • Beginners often ghost too many ups — don't be afraid to let them ring
Up strum isolation exercise — before full patterns Hold Em chord. Set metronome 50 BPM. Bar 1 : ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ (all 8 eighth notes — train the wrist motion) Bar 2 : ↓ · ↓ · ↓ · ↓ · (downstrokes only — compare the feel) Bar 3 : · ↑ · ↑ · ↑ · ↑ (upstrokes only — isolate the motion) Bar 4 : ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ (all 8 again — now feel the smoothness) Key: the wrist never stops between bars 1 and 4. Only the contact changes.

L-08 All About Capos Tool

A capo is a clamp that attaches to the neck of your guitar, pressing all strings down at a specific fret. It effectively raises the pitch of all strings by one semitone per fret — allowing you to use the same chord shapes you know but in a different key. It's one of the most useful tools in acoustic guitar.

Why capos matter for you right now: Many Bollywood and Western songs are recorded in keys that require barre chords or unusual chord shapes. A capo lets you play the same D, A, E, Em, Am shapes you already know and sound exactly like the recording. Most beginner-friendly song arrangements use a capo for this reason.

How to use a capo — basics

  • Clamp the capo on the desired fret — directly behind the fret wire, not on top of it
  • Check that all strings ring cleanly under the capo — if any buzz, reposition
  • Capo fret 1 = all chords sound 1 semitone higher than normal
  • Capo fret 2 = all chords sound 1 full step (2 semitones) higher
  • Think of it as moving the nut of the guitar up the neck
  • Your chord shapes and finger positions stay exactly the same

Capo key chart — D, A, E shapes

  • No capo: D = D · A = A · E = E
  • Capo 1: D = D# · A = A# · E = F
  • Capo 2: D = E · A = B · E = F# (very common)
  • Capo 3: D = F · A = C · E = G (makes D shape sound like F — no barre needed!)
  • Capo 4: D = F# · A = C# · E = G#
  • Capo 5: D = G · A = D · E = A (the entire set shifts up perfectly)

L-09 Seven Nation Army Second Riff

Seven Nation Army (The White Stripes, 2003) is the second great beginner riff and one of the most recognisable guitar lines in modern music. Like Peter Gunn, it is a single-string riff — played on the low E string, but now descending and with a distinctive rhythmic feel. Sports arenas worldwide chant it. You'll recognise it the moment you play the first four notes.

Why this riff matters: Peter Gunn was ascending and taught you single-string accuracy. Seven Nation Army descends and introduces rhythmic articulation — specifically the triplet feel in the opening, and the importance of rhythm in riff identity. The notes of Seven Nation Army are almost secondary to the rhythm — it's the groove that makes it iconic.

Play slowly at first — the rhythm is: long · short · long · long · short · short · rest Fret guide: 5=ring or pinky, 7=pinky (stretch), 10=barre or pinky (5 frets up from 5th)
Rhythm notation — Seven Nation Army (simplified) Rhythm : LONG short LONG LONG short short rest Count : 1 - & - 2 - 3 - & - 4 & - rest Note 7 : LONG (holds for almost 2 beats) Note 7 : short (quick) Note 10 : LONG Note 7 : LONG Note 5 : short Note 7 : short Note 5 : LONG (rings out) Tip: Sing the rhythm before playing. "DA-da DA-DA-da-da rest" — feel it before you fret it.

Learning steps — Seven Nation Army

  • Step 1: Tap the rhythm on your leg first — get the long-short-long pattern in your body
  • Step 2: Play only notes 7-7-10 (first three notes) until the rhythm is right
  • Step 3: Add 7-5 (notes 4 and 5) — this descent is the heart of the riff
  • Step 4: Add 7-5 at the end (notes 6 and 7) to complete the phrase
  • Step 5: Loop the full riff with a pause before repeating — groove over speed
  • Fret 10 option: use a half-barre at fret 10 or stretch from fret 7 with pinky

What this riff teaches

  • Rhythm is the message — the notes are secondary to the groove
  • Descending phrases: different feel and fingering to ascending riffs
  • Pinky training: fret 10 requires the 4th finger or a significant stretch
  • Note duration: long and short notes express different emotions — feel the difference
  • The "repeat riff" song structure — most rock and blues is built on this principle
  • Playing for a reaction: people recognise this riff instantly — first audience moment

L-11 Module 3 Practice Routine Structure

Module 3 has the most new content so far — two new chords, up strums, four new strum patterns, capo technique, and a new riff. Resist the urge to practice everything every day. Instead, rotate focus: rhythm one day, chords the next, song on the third. This prevents plateau and builds all skills simultaneously.

Module 3 completion checklist

  • Em — all 6 strings ring clean; can switch E → Em in under 1 second
  • Am — all 5 strings ring clean including open high e
  • Em ↔ Am OMC: 25+ changes per minute
  • Am ↔ D OMC: 25+ changes per minute

  • Counting ands: can count "1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and" in time with a song

  • Seven Nation Army riff: full riff at 80 BPM with correct rhythm (long-short-long)
  • Complete song using Em or Am — verse and chorus with consistent strum pattern

Module 3 — common mistakes

MistakeFix
Em high e and B strings buzzingMiddle and ring fingers are not arching enough — only the very tips should touch the A and D strings. Practice with finger isolation: hold each finger alone and verify the open strings still ring.
Am high e string mutingIndex finger (B-str fret 1) is too flat and touching the high e. Arch the index more — the tip should press the B string while the pad clears the high e.
Up strums catching all 6 strings (harsh)Reduce the travel distance of your upstroke. The up strum should catch only the top 2–3 strings. Practice the upstroke alone, aiming to miss the low E and A strings entirely.
DDUUDU pattern — losing beat 3 upstrokeThe "3 ↑" in DDUUDU is on the beat (not the "and"), which feels wrong at first. Count aloud: "1 and 2 and UP and 3+and UP and" — the word "UP" marks beats 3 and 4 of the pattern.
Capo causing string buzzCapo is not close enough to the fret wire. Move it as close to the metal fret as possible without sitting on top of it. Also check that the capo applies equal pressure across all strings — it may be slightly crooked.
Seven Nation Army rhythm wrong — sounds like notes, not a riffYou're making all notes equal length. The riff is defined by its LONG-short rhythm. Sing it first: "DA-da-DA-DA-da-da-rest." The first note holds for nearly two beats. If it doesn't groove, slow down and exaggerate the long notes.
Not connecting patterns to songsAlways practice strum patterns on a song, not in isolation. Four bars of a real song with Pattern 3 teaches timing, feel, and chord changes simultaneously. Isolated strumming practice has a ceiling.
Ready for Module 4? Module 4 introduces the F chord — the first "difficult" chord in the course and a major milestone. F requires a partial barre (index finger across strings 1 and 2), which takes several weeks to feel comfortable. Starting it early is wise. The reward: F unlocks the full Am–F–C–G progression that powers the majority of popular music worldwide.
