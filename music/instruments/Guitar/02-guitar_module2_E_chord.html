L-01 How To Play The E Chord Foundation

E major is one of the most important open chords in guitar — it uses all 6 strings, rings with massive resonance, and underpins hundreds of Bollywood and Western songs. If D was your first chord and A was your second, E completes the holy trinity of beginner guitar.

E MajorOpen · All 6 Strings · Full Resonance

6 (Low E)OpenOERoot · deep bass
5 (A)22BMiddle finger
4 (D)23ERing finger
3 (G)11G#Index finger
2 (B)OpenOBOpen ring
1 (High e)OpenOEOpen ring
Strum all 6 strings — unlike D and A which avoid the low strings, E uses everything. Start from the low E string (string 6) and sweep down through all strings in one motion.

E chord — key technique points

  • Index finger (1) on G string fret 1 — must be very close to the metal fret wire
  • Middle (2) and ring (3) fingers on A and D strings fret 2 — side by side, arched
  • Three open strings ring freely — low E, B, high e
  • Thumb must sit behind the neck — not gripping over the top
  • All 6 strings should ring — pluck each one individually to verify

The E chord family — why this matters now

  • E major is the shape all barre chords are built from (Module 5)
  • Slide this shape up 2 frets with a barre = F# major
  • Slide to fret 5 = A major (barre version)
  • Understanding E now makes barre chords logical, not mysterious
  • E minor is even easier — just remove finger 1 (index)
  • E minor appears in Tum Hi Ho, Labon Ko, Shape of You
L-02 E Chord Anchors & Tricks Technique

Now you have three chords. The key to making them sound musical — not mechanical — is understanding which fingers can stay put (or nearly) during each change. Every chord transition has a hidden shortcut.

The A → E anchor trick: When changing from A to E, your middle (2) and ring (3) fingers both stay on fret 2 — they just shift from the D and G strings to the A and D strings. Only your index finger moves (from D-str to G-str). This makes A → E one of the fastest chord changes in beginner guitar once you notice the anchor.
All Three Anchor Relationships — Module 2 Summary D → A : Middle finger anchors near fret 2 area · index and ring move A → E : Middle + Ring stay on fret 2, shift one string · Index is the only big move E → D : No common anchor · practice as pure OMC — this is the hardest change A → D : Index finger stays near fret 2 · Ring moves to B string E → A : Ring stays on D fret 2 · Middle shifts string · Index lifts

The 2-finger A chord trick

  • Struggling to fit 3 fingers on the A chord? Use your middle AND ring only
  • Middle on D string fret 2, ring on G string fret 2 — skip the B string
  • It sounds slightly thin but perfectly usable in most songs
  • More importantly: both fingers stay in position for E and D transitions
  • Upgrade to 3-finger A once your finger calluses are fully formed (3–5 weeks)

E → D: the hard change — how to drill it

  • This is the hardest change in Module 2 — no clean anchor exists
  • From E: release all, form D with index on G-str, ring on B-str, middle on e-str
  • Drill OMC on E ↔ D specifically — 3 sets daily until it reaches 25+ per minute
  • Focus on index finger finding G-str fret 2 first — the other fingers follow
  • In songs, E → D often has 4 beats — you have time if you start moving early
L-03 Tapping Your Foot Rhythm

Foot tapping is not a beginner crutch — professional musicians tap, sway, bob, and feel the pulse physically. Your body is the metronome. Training your foot to keep steady time separates rhythmic players from mechanical ones, and it enables up-strumming because the foot subdivides the beat automatically.

The core principle: Your foot taps DOWN on every beat (1, 2, 3, 4) and comes UP on every "and" (+). When you strum down on the beat, your foot is going down. When you strum up on the "and", your foot is coming up. The foot and the strum move in the same direction at the same time. This is the physical lock that makes rhythm feel natural.

Foot and strum relationship — 4/4 time

↓Beat 1foot ↓
·1 +foot ↑
↓Beat 2foot ↓
·2 +foot ↑
↓Beat 3foot ↓
·3 +foot ↑
↓Beat 4foot ↓
·4 +foot ↑

Training foot tap independently

  • Step 1: Put the guitar down. Just tap your foot to a song you know well — feel the pulse
  • Step 2: Count aloud with the tap: "1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and" — foot hits on numbers
  • Step 3: Pick up guitar, hold a chord, tap foot, strum ONLY on foot-down (quarter notes)
  • Step 4: Gradually add the "and" strums — foot guides when they happen
  • Step 5: The foot should never stop moving once you start — it is the pulse engine

Why foot tapping transforms your rhythm

  • You cannot rush or drag if your foot is steady — it's a physical correction system
  • Chord changes become timed to the foot, not to when your fingers are ready
  • Up-strums become natural — when your foot lifts, your hand lifts with it
  • You can feel when something is wrong before you hear it
  • Live performance: your foot keeps you locked in even when distracted
Common error — stopping the foot on chord changes: Beginners often freeze the foot when changing chords. The foot must keep going even when the strum hand pauses. Practice tapping your foot continuously for 60 seconds without stopping — then add the guitar.
L-04 Strumming Mechanics Technique

This lesson zooms in on the physical mechanics of a real strum. Module 1 introduced pure downstrokes — now we dissect exactly how the arm, wrist, and pick interact to produce a strum that sounds musical rather than mechanical.

The anatomy of a downstroke

  • Forearm rests lightly on the guitar body — this is the pivot point
  • The motion comes from the wrist rotating, NOT from the elbow lifting
  • Imagine shaking water off your fingertips — that loose downward flick is the motion
  • Pick angle: roughly 45° to the strings — angled slightly away from the body
  • Pick depth: the pick brushes across the strings, touching each one briefly
  • The pick should never "dig in" — think of it as skimming the surface

The anatomy of an upstroke

  • The wrist rotates back upward after the downstroke — not a separate motion
  • On the upstroke, typically only strings 1–3 (high strings) are caught — this is correct
  • A full upstroke catching all 6 strings sounds harsh and is wrong
  • Pick angle flips slightly on the upstroke — the other face of the pick leads
  • Upstrokes are naturally lighter in volume than downstrokes — this is correct feel
  • Practice the up motion slowly while holding a chord and NOT strumming
The pendulum image: Your strumming arm is a pendulum. Once it starts swinging, it keeps swinging — down, up, down, up — at a steady tempo. The question is not "when do I strum" but "when do I let the pendulum hit the strings." Sometimes you strum on the down swing, sometimes on the up swing, sometimes you ghost through the strings without touching them (ghost strum). The pendulum never stops.
Ghost Strum Concept — The Invisible Beat Keeper A ghost strum = the strumming hand moves in the correct direction at the correct time but does not make contact with the strings. Example : D · D · D · D · Hand motion: ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ (always moving) Sound : STRUM · STRUM · STRUM · STRUM (only on ↓ beats) Ghost : ↑ moves happen but don't touch strings — this keeps time AND sets up upstrokes

Pick holding — common errors fixed

  • Gripping too tight: pick snags on strings, breaks the flow — loosen your grip by 50%
  • Pick perpendicular to strings: creates harsh, scratchy tone — angle it 45°
  • Too much pick exposed: only 3–5mm of pick tip should protrude beyond finger
  • Pick flopping around: pinch firmly between thumb pad and index finger side
  • No pick: using thumb is fine — use the nail side on downstrokes, fleshy side on up

Tone check — does your strum sound right?

  • All strings should have roughly equal volume — no single string louder
  • The sound should be smooth and connected, not a series of individual plucks
  • Downstroke should have slight natural accent — this is correct
  • No scratching or pick-dragging sound — if present, reduce pick depth
  • Record 30 seconds of strumming and listen back — you will hear issues you can't feel

L-06 Chord Changes For One Application

"Changes for one" means you strum just once per chord — one down strum, change chord, one down strum, repeat. This strips away pattern complexity so you can focus entirely on the timing of chord changes. It is the bridge between isolated chord drills and real song playing.

Changes For One — Protocol Tempo : Start at 50 BPM Pattern: ONE down strum per chord (one strum = one beat, or 2 beats, depending on the song) Goal : The chord change must be COMPLETED BEFORE the next strum Process: Strum D (beat 1) → move fingers immediately after strumming → form A → strum A (beat 2) Key : The strum is a deadline. Your fingers must meet the deadline. Advance: Increase BPM by 5 each time you play 8 bars without a late change

Changes for one — progressions to drill

  • D → A → D → A (2 beats each at 60 BPM)
  • A → E → A → E (2 beats each at 60 BPM)
  • D → E → D → E (2 beats each — hardest pair)
  • D → A → E → D (1 bar each — the full triangle)
  • E → A → D → E (reverse triangle — adds E → D)
  • Mix: D D A A E E A A (real song feel — 2 beats each chord)

Why "for one" works better than OMC at this stage

  • OMC trains speed. Changes-for-one trains timing — a different skill
  • Playing to a beat forces you to hit the change ON TIME, not just quickly
  • Early changes (before the beat) sound wrong — you learn to feel the deadline
  • Late changes (after the beat) sound worse — the gap is audible
  • Songs use changes-for-one timing naturally — this is direct song preparation
  • Once clean at 80 BPM, add Pattern 1 strumming on top
The mental shift: Strum first, then change. Beginners instinctively want to form the chord, then strum it. Flip this: strum the chord you're on (even if it's slightly wrong), then change. The strum is the beat marker; the chord is built around the beat, not before it.
L-07 Peter Gunn Theme First Riff

The Peter Gunn Theme (Henry Mancini, 1958) is one of the most famous bass riffs in music history — made famous again by Ray Charles, Blues Brothers, and countless film soundtracks. It is a perfect first riff because it uses a single string, sits entirely on the low E string, and sounds immediately impressive even to non-guitarists.

Why this riff? Up to now everything has been chord strumming. The Peter Gunn riff introduces single-note playing (lead guitar technique) using the same fingers you've already trained. It's rewarding, recognisable, and introduces the concept of playing rhythmically on a single string — the foundation of all riff playing and eventually blues and rock lead.
Peter Gunn Theme — Low E String — Standard Tuning e |-------------------------------------------------------| B |-------------------------------------------------------| G |-------------------------------------------------------| D |-------------------------------------------------------| A |-------------------------------------------------------| E |0 0 3 0 0 0 5 4 0 0 3 0 2 1 0------| All notes on the low E string (string 6). Numbers = fret numbers. 0 = open string. Rhythm: Each group of 4 notes fills one bar of 4/4. Play with steady down-picking.
Fingering Guide — Peter Gunn Riff Fret 0 → Open Low E — no fretting needed Fret 1 → Index finger (1) Fret 2 → Middle finger (2) Fret 3 → Ring finger (3) Fret 4 → Pinky (4) — or ring finger stretch Fret 5 → Pinky (4) Tip: Keep fingers close to the fretboard even when not fretting — reduces travel time. Tip: Play each note with firm, clean pressure — muted notes will buzz. Tip: Practice the second half (0 0 3 0 / 2 1 0) first — it's the tricky descent.

Learning steps — Peter Gunn

  • Step 1: Play just fret 0, 0, 3, 0 repeatedly until it grooves
  • Step 2: Add the second group: 0, 0, 5, 4
  • Step 3: Repeat groups 1 and 2 together as a loop
  • Step 4: Add the descent: 0, 0, 3, 0, 2, 1, 0
  • Step 5: Full riff — slow at first, then build speed
  • Target tempo: 80–100 BPM with a steady groove feel

What this riff teaches you

  • Single-string accuracy — picking one string cleanly without touching others
  • Left-hand muting — fingers not fretting must hover and mute nearby strings
  • Rhythmic feel on single notes — different skill from chord strumming
  • The concept of a riff: a repeated melodic/rhythmic figure that defines a song
  • Introduction to the pinky (4th finger) — most beginners avoid it; this forces its use

L-09 Module 2 Practice Routine Structure

Module 2 introduces more moving parts than Module 1 — foot tapping, up-strums, the E chord, and the Peter Gunn riff all need dedicated time. The key is keeping sessions under 35 minutes so they stay sustainable. Longer sessions plateau; consistent short sessions compound.

Use your phone, even just voice memo. Compare recordings week over week — the improvement will surprise and motivate you.

Module 2 completion checklist

  • E chord — all 6 strings ring cleanly when plucked individually
  • A → E anchor identified and used consistently — middle + ring fingers stay on fret 2
  • Foot tap — can tap steady quarter-note pulse for 2 minutes without stopping
  • Pattern 1 (quarter downs) at 80 BPM — 8 bars clean with foot tap
  • Pattern 3 (DDUUDU) at 65 BPM — 4 bars without losing the beat
  • A ↔ E OMC: 25+ changes per minute
  • D ↔ E OMC: 20+ changes per minute
  • Changes for one — D → A → E triangle at 70 BPM, 4 bars without a late change
  • Peter Gunn riff — full riff at 80 BPM, notes all clean
  • One complete song from the Module 2 list — verse and chorus with consistent strum
  • Song recorded on phone — changes happen in the right places

Common Module 2 mistakes to avoid

MistakeFix
E chord — high e and B strings mutingIndex finger (on G-str fret 1) is arching too low and touching B string. Raise the arch — only the very tip of the index should touch the G string.
Stopping the foot during chord changesConsciously tap your foot for 30 seconds before picking up the guitar. Train the foot as an independent habit, then add the guitar on top.
Up-strums hitting all 6 strings (too heavy)Up-strums are high-string only — reduce the distance your pick travels upward. Think of catching only strings 1–3 on the upstroke.
DDUUDU pattern losing the beat on the "3 ↑"The beat-3 upstroke is the hardest subdivision. Practice just beats 2+ → 3 → 3+ in isolation. Count aloud: "two-and THREE-and" until it grooves.
Peter Gunn — fret buzz on notes 3, 4, 5Fingers are not pressing close enough to the fret wire. Move each finger as close to the metal fret as possible without going over it.
Chord changes for one — arriving lateStart moving your fretting fingers IMMEDIATELY after the strum — not after you hear the sound. The change begins on the strum, not after it.
Rushing during exciting parts of a songFoot tap is the cure — if the foot is steady, the strum follows. When you feel excited, slow the foot consciously.

Module 2 progress metrics

OMC scores to log weekly

Ready for Module 3? Module 3 introduces G and C — the two chords that complete the most common beginner progression (G · C · D · Em). With those four chords you can play virtually any pop, Bollywood, folk, or rock song at a basic level. The jump in your song range when you add G and C is the biggest single leap in the entire course.
