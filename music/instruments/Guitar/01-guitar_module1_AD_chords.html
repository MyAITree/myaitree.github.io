L-01 The D Chord Foundation

The D chord is most beginners' very first chord — it uses only four strings, sounds bright and full, and appears in an enormous number of songs. Master it before moving to A.

D MajorOpen · 4 Strings Only

Strum only strings 4 through 1. The D chord is a 4-string chord. Accidentally hitting the A or low E string creates a muddy, wrong-sounding chord. Practice aiming your strum to start exactly at string 4 (the D string).

Common beginner problems

  • Fingers not pressing close enough to the fret — buzzing sound results
  • Accidentally muting the open D string with your palm
  • Strumming the low E or A string — always start from D string
  • Fingers touching adjacent strings and muting them
  • Not arching the fingers — use the tips, not the flat pad

Practice steps — in order

  • Place fingers one at a time and check each note rings
  • Pluck strings individually (fingerpick test) before strumming
  • Hold chord and strum down slowly — 4 beats
  • Release and reform — aim for under 10 seconds initially
  • Work toward forming in 3 seconds, then 1 second
Wrist angle tip: Your fretting hand wrist should curve out from the neck so your thumb sits behind the middle finger on the back of the neck — not gripping over the top. This lets your fingers arch naturally and avoids muting adjacent strings.
L-02 The A Chord Foundation

The A chord is your second chord. Like D, it's an open chord with a clear, bright sound. It uses 5 strings and introduces a three-fingers-on-one-fret shape that some beginners find challenging — but there's a simpler two-finger variant to begin with.

A MajorOpen · 5 Strings

The A chord places three fingers side by side on the second fret — D, G, and B strings. This is tight! Fingers must arch so they don't accidentally mute the open high e string.

Beginner shortcut: You can also form A with just your index and middle finger — index on D fret 2, middle on G fret 2. Skip the B string for now. It won't sound perfect, but it lets you focus on transitions first.

A chord — key tips

  • Three fingers squeezed on fret 2 — keep them very close to the metal fret wire
  • High e must ring open — if it mutes, your ring finger is too flat
  • Thumb stays behind the neck, NOT hooked over the top
  • Alternative: use index finger as a small barre across D, G, B on fret 2
  • Strum from the A string (string 5) — not the low E

D → A: your first change

  • Both chords use strings in the same fret area — this helps
  • From D, your index finger shifts from G-fret2 to D-fret2
  • Think about where the anchor point is between the two chords
  • This change appears in hundreds of songs — practice it daily
  • Start at 40 BPM and increase only when clean

L-04 E Chord Introduction New Chord

The E major chord is one of the most powerful open chords — it uses all 6 strings and creates a full, rich, resonant sound. It also shares finger shapes with A and D, making transitions between them easier than they look.

E MajorOpen · All 6 Strings

The magic of E: Strum all 6 strings — E major is one of the fullest-sounding open chords. It is also the basis for barre chords later. Notice that A and E share almost the same finger shape — just shifted across strings. This is the key insight for the next lesson on anchor fingers.

D → A → E progression

  • These three chords together unlock an enormous number of songs
  • Bollywood: Tum Hi Ho (Aashiqui 2), Tera Hone Laga Hoon
  • Western: Wonderwall (Oasis with capo), Brown Eyed Girl (Van Morrison)
  • Practice as: D (4 beats) → A (4 beats) → E (4 beats), loop
  • Then try: D (2 beats) → A (2 beats) → E (4 beats)

E → A relationship (crucial insight)

  • E chord: index on G-str fret 1, mid on A-str fret 2, ring on D-str fret 2
  • A chord: index on D-str fret 2, mid on G-str fret 2, ring on B-str fret 2
  • Notice the shape shifts one string down from E to A
  • This string-shifting principle underpins all barre chord theory
  • Understanding this now pays off enormously at Module 5+
L-05 Anchor Fingers Technique

An anchor finger is a finger that stays on the same string and fret (or near it) when you change chords. Instead of lifting all fingers and starting fresh, you keep one finger down and move the others around it. This dramatically reduces transition time.

D Chord
→
A Chord
D to A anchor: When moving from D to A, your middle finger (finger 2) stays near the 2nd fret area. Don't lift it — glide it from the high e string to the G string. This gliding motion is the anchor. Practice lifting finger 1 and finger 3 independently while keeping finger 2 hovering close to its position.

Common anchor relationships

  • D → A: middle finger anchors around fret 2 area
  • A → E: ring finger anchors from D-str-fret2 area
  • E → A: index finger anchors from G-str-fret1 area
  • Any chord change: identify which finger moves the least — that's your anchor
  • Even if no finger stays in place, the finger that moves least is the psychological anchor

Training the anchor

  • Isolate the anchor: hold only the anchor finger on its position
  • While holding, place the other fingers of chord 1
  • Strum chord 1 (4 beats)
  • While keeping the anchor, remove only the moving fingers
  • Place them in chord 2 position — strum chord 2
  • Repeat — only the non-anchor fingers should be moving
L-06 One-Minute Changes Core Exercise

One-Minute Changes (OMC) is the most effective chord-transition exercise for beginners. The concept is simple: set a timer for 60 seconds, change between two chords as many times as possible, and count. Track your score daily — improvement is measurable and motivating.

One-Minute Changes — Protocol 1. Choose two chords (e.g. D and A) 2. Set a timer for 60 seconds 3. Hold chord 1 → strum once → immediately change to chord 2 → strum once → repeat 4. Count every complete change (each time you return to chord 1 = 1 point) 5. Write your score in your log 6. Rest 60 seconds — repeat 2 more times 7. Move to next pair only when you hit your target score

Target scores

  • Week 1: 10+ changes per minute — foundation
  • Week 2: 20+ changes — beginning to flow
  • Week 3: 30+ changes — songs becoming possible
  • Week 4: 40+ changes — ready for real song tempo
  • Advanced target: 60+ changes — one change per second
  • Move on when you hit 30+ comfortably for 3 days in a row

OMC pairs for this module

  • D ↔ A — primary pair, practice every day
  • A ↔ E — secondary pair, add Week 2
  • D ↔ E — add Week 2 alongside A ↔ E
  • D → A → E → D — triangle drill (Week 3+)
  • Do each pair minimum 3 sets of 1 minute daily
  • Total OMC time per session: 12–15 minutes
Important: Speed is secondary to accuracy in OMC. A clean but slow change scores zero if the chord buzzes or mutes. Always verify the chord rings cleanly before counting the change. Slow clean changes build faster than fast sloppy ones.

L-07 Down Strumming Rhythm

Before learning complex strumming patterns, master the pure downstroke. A clean, controlled down strum is the foundation of all rhythm guitar — Bollywood, folk, pop, rock. Consistency and timing matter more than speed at this stage.

The wrist is everything: A proper strum comes from a loose, rotating wrist — not from the elbow or the arm. Anchor your forearm lightly on the guitar body. The motion should feel like you're shaking water off your hand. The pick or thumb should brush across all target strings in one fluid motion.

Strumming technique checklist

  • Wrist loose — not stiff. If your arm aches, you're using the wrong muscles
  • Pick angle: about 45° to the strings — not perpendicular
  • Pick depth: brush across, don't dig in — light contact only
  • Consistent volume across all strings — no single string dominating
  • Keep the strumming hand moving even on rests (ghost strums)
  • Use a metronome — without it, your tempo will drift without you noticing

Common strumming errors

  • Elbow-driving the strum — creates stiff, robotic sound
  • Gripping the pick too hard — kills the natural feel
  • Rushing beat 4 — the most common timing error in beginners
  • Strumming too deeply into the strings — creates harsh sound
  • Not keeping the arm moving on rests — breaks the groove feel
  • Speeding up on easy parts, slowing on hard ones — always use a metronome
Metronome practice: Start at 60 BPM for Pattern 1. Only increase by 5 BPM when the pattern is absolutely clean and relaxed. Tension is a signal to slow down. Target 80–90 BPM clean before finishing this module.
L-08 Beginner Song Course Introduction Context

Songs are the destination — everything else is preparation. From this lesson onward, your chord work, OMC scores, and strumming patterns exist to serve real music. Here is how to approach learning a song at this stage.

Song Learning Framework — 4 Steps Step 1 · Listen → Listen to the song 3+ times. Identify the chord changes by ear — where do they happen? On which beat? Sections (verse, chorus)? Step 2 · Map → Write out the chord sequence: Verse: D · D · A · A | Chorus: E · A · D · D Step 3 · Drill → Practice OMC on the pairs that appear in the song before attempting the full song Step 4 · Play → Start with just the chord changes (no strumming) in time with the song. Then add basic down strums.

What makes a song "beginner friendly"

  • 3 or fewer chords (D, A, E covers an enormous repertoire)
  • Slow or medium tempo — time to make changes
  • Simple strumming pattern or even just one strum per chord
  • Long chord holds — 4+ beats per chord at first
  • Well-known chorus to motivate practicing through hard spots
  • Capo options — many songs become easier with a capo on fret 2 or 4

Guitar myths to ignore right now

  • "I need to learn all chords first" — false. D, A, E unlock hundreds of songs
  • "My fingers hurt too much to practice" — calluses form in 3–4 weeks
  • "I need to learn to read music" — tab and chord charts are enough at this stage
  • "I'm not musically gifted" — all beginners sound bad; it's a phase, not a trait
  • "I should learn classical technique first" — direct to songs is better for motivation

L-10 Practice Routine Structure

A structured 20–30 minute daily session builds faster than sporadic 1-hour sessions. Consistency beats volume. Here are two options — a minimal 20-minute routine and a full 35-minute routine.

Daily 20-Minute Routine (Minimum)

2 min Warmup + Stretch
5 min Chord Forming (D, A, E individually)
8 min OMC — 2 pairs × 3 sets × 1 min
5 min Song Practice (1 song, chorus loop)

Full 35-Minute Routine (Recommended)

3 min Warmup · Finger Stretch
5 min Chord Forming + Clean Note Check
12 min OMC — 3 pairs × 3 sets × 1 min + 1 min rest
5 min Strumming Pattern Drill w/ Metronome
10 min Song Practice — Full song run-through
The 3-day rule: If you miss a day, play the very next day. Missing two consecutive days breaks the habit loop and costs 3–4 days of progress. Fifteen minutes on a tired evening beats zero.

L-11 Progress Tracking Self-Assessment

Tracking progress makes improvement visible and keeps motivation alive. Log the numbers below weekly — improvements in OMC score and song smoothness are the clearest signals of real progress.

OMC Log — Weekly Best

Record your single best score for D ↔ A each week. A rise of 5+ per week is excellent progress. If you plateau for 2 weeks, slow down and focus on clean chord formation before speed.

Chord Quality Check

Once per week: pluck every string individually on D, A, and E. Count how many ring clean vs. mute/buzz. Target: all 4 (D) and 5 (A, E) strings ringing clean before the module assessment.

Song Run-Throughs

Record a 60-second clip of your song on your phone weekly. Listen back. You will hear improvement faster than you feel it. This is the most motivating form of tracking available.

Weekly Checklist — Module 1

  • D chord — all 4 strings ring clean when plucked individually
  • A chord — all 5 strings ring clean including high e open
  • E chord — all 6 strings ring clean
  • D ↔ A OMC score: 30+ per minute for 3 consecutive days
  • A ↔ E OMC score: 20+ per minute
  • D ↔ E OMC score: 20+ per minute
  • Down strum — 4 quarter notes at 80 BPM, consistent volume and tone
  • Can explain anchor finger concept for D ↔ A
  • One complete song played from start to finish (any song from Lesson 9 list)
  • Song recorded on phone — sounds recognisable
L-12 Move On Assessment Milestone

Before moving to Module 2, confirm you can meet the following criteria. These are not high bars — they are the honest minimum needed to benefit from Module 2 content. Attempting Module 2 without meeting them creates frustration, not progress.

Assessment 1 — Chord Quality

Strum D chord. Strum A chord. Strum E chord. All strings in each chord must ring cleanly — no mutes, no buzzes.

Target: 3/3 chords clean
Assessment 2 — OMC Score

D ↔ A: 30+ changes per minute. A ↔ E: 20+ changes per minute. Both measured on a single fresh attempt.

Target: 30+ D↔A · 20+ A↔E
Assessment 3 — Strumming Timing

Set metronome to 70 BPM. Strum D chord — 4 quarter-note down strums per bar. Hold for 8 bars without losing the beat.

Target: 8 bars clean at 70 BPM
Assessment 4 — Song Performance

Play any song from Lesson 9 from beginning to end. Chord changes must happen in approximately the right places — perfection not required.

Target: 1 full song, start to finish
If you pass all four: You are ready for Module 2 — which introduces G and C chords, up-strumming, and the full down-up pattern. The jump in song range when you add G and C is enormous — Wonderwall, Tum Hi Ho complete version, Let Her Go complete version, and hundreds more become fully accessible.
If you don't pass yet: This is completely normal and expected. Most beginners need 4–6 weeks on Module 1. Go back to the specific area that needs work. OMC scores plateau then jump. Do not move to Module 2 until you feel genuinely confident — not just technically meeting the criteria.
